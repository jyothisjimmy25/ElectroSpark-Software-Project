Bridge Rectifier

Bridge rectifier uses 4 rectifying diodes connected in a "bridged" configuration to produce the desired output but does not require a special center-tapped transformer, thereby reducing its size and cost compared to center-tapped rectifier.

Basic Theory

A bridge rectifier circuit is a simple yet effective way to convert alternating current (AC) into direct current (DC). It consists of four diodes arranged in a bridge configuration. Here's a simplified theoretical explanation of how it works:

Basic Principle: The fundamental principle behind a bridge rectifier is the use of diodes to allow the flow of current in only one direction. Diodes are electronic components that conduct current in one direction and block it in the opposite direction.

AC Input: The bridge rectifier circuit is typically connected to an AC power source, such as the output of a transformer. AC power alternates between positive and negative cycles.

Diode Operation: In the bridge rectifier, diodes are arranged in such a way that during each half-cycle of the AC input, two diodes conduct current. These diodes provide a conductive path for the current to flow towards the load.

Rectification: During the positive half-cycle of the AC input, the diodes D1 and D2 conduct, allowing the current to flow through them and into the load in the same direction. Meanwhile, diodes D3 and D4 are reverse-biased and do not conduct. Similarly, during the negative half-cycle of the AC input, diodes D3 and D4 conduct, allowing current to flow in the same direction through the load, while D1 and D2 are reverse-biased.

Output: As a result of this arrangement, the output across the load is a pulsating DC waveform. Although the output voltage is not perfectly smooth, it predominantly flows in one direction, effectively converting the AC input into DC.

Smoothing: To obtain a smoother DC output, a filter capacitor is often added across the load. This capacitor helps to reduce the ripple voltage by storing charge during peak periods and discharging during the troughs, resulting in a more stable DC output voltage.

In summary, a bridge rectifier circuit utilizes the property of diodes to allow current flow in only one direction, thereby converting AC input into pulsating DC output.
